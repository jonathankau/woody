# Woody Word-Pair Rubric

Every pair must be evaluated as a playable round, not merely as two related terms.
The target table is 5-8 adults, with enough room for at least two clue rounds.

## Acceptance Test

A pair ships only when it passes all six checks:

1. **Standalone clarity**: each term makes immediate sense without setup or an
   implied situation. Prefer concrete nouns, familiar activities, and plain verbs.
2. **Shared comparison axis**: both terms are naturally compared in the same way.
   Being associated, sequential, or found in the same broad category is not enough.
3. **Comparable specificity**: neither side is obscure, technical, or much narrower
   than the other. A category/example pair is allowed only when the example is
   prototypical and both sides still support overlapping clues.
4. **Clue overlap**: several honest clues could plausibly describe either term.
   Players should be able to stay subtle without resorting to generic filler.
5. **Useful distinction**: the terms are not synonyms. Later clues can separate
   them, and an undercover has a fair chance to infer the relationship.
6. **Audience fit**: the term belongs in the selected pack and is recognizable to
   the intended audience. General Mix must avoid workplace jargon and niche terms.

## Instant Recognition

Read each term alone for one second. At least 90% of the pack's intended audience
should immediately know what it means, and concrete terms should produce a clear
mental image without additional context. General Mix should clear an even higher
bar: familiar to nearly any US adult.

- Prefer the common name over the precise subtype: `piano`, not `upright piano`.
- Avoid interface taxonomy such as `recommended feed` or `chronological feed`.
- Avoid technically correct words that are uncommon in conversation, even when
  the object itself is familiar.
- Avoid modifier-heavy phrases that must be parsed before they can be pictured.
- Specialized packs may use culturally specific terms only when the intended
  audience is very likely to recognize them immediately.
- The challenge should come from comparing the two words, never from decoding one.

If a reviewer cannot quickly imagine three fair clues for each side, reject or
rewrite the pair.

## Wording

- Prefer one or two words.
- Use three or more words only for a widely recognized proper noun or fixed phrase.
- Avoid contextual constructions such as `grocery run`, `tailor visit`, `allergy
  note`, or `dessert option`.
- Avoid unnecessary modifiers when the shorter term is unambiguous.
- Activities and verbs are welcome when both sides describe comparable actions,
  such as `hiking / camping` or `whisper / shout`.

## Strong Pair Shapes

- Peer objects: `fork / spoon`, `candle / flashlight`
- Peer places: `library / bookstore`, `aquarium / zoo`
- Peer activities: `hiking / camping`, `skiing / snowboarding`
- Peer verbs: `whisper / mumble`, `borrow / lend`
- Neighboring concepts on one clear axis: `fog / mist`, `movie / TV show`
- Careful category/example: `fruit / apple`, only when the overlap remains playable

## Automatic Rejections

- Broad-category adjacency: `yogurt parfait / hash browns`
- Mere co-occurrence: `mailbox / front door`
- Sequence or workflow adjacency: `boarding pass / security line`
- Different semantic roles: `raincheck / RSVP`
- Visual resemblance without shared use: `lighthouse / water tower`
- Specialist vocabulary in General Mix: `trailhead`
- Slow-to-parse vocabulary: `recommended feed`, `upright piano`, `splash pad`
- Pack leakage: office and school material in General Mix

## Review Labels

- **Keep**: clearly passes every check.
- **Rewrite**: the concept is useful but one or both terms need replacement.
- **Remove**: the matchup does not produce a fair, interesting round.

Reviewers should be conservative. Repetition of a strong semantic shape is better
than novelty that creates confusion, but exact pairs must remain unique within a
pack.
